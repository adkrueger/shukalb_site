Arlinda Ibrahimllari

Albania - Getting her start in the Polytechnic University of Tirana, Arlinda Ibrahimllari engaged in the new branch of environmental studies, such as those in air pollution, water supply, and wastewater. She later obtained her MBA in economics and completed her post-graduate degree in environmental engineering in Japan.

The school was male-dominated, and it was difficult to find any women studying engineering at the time. However, Ibrahimllari viewed this as an opportunity. With only a few women working in the wastewater sector, Ibrahimllari realized she had a great chance to pursue her passion and gain visibility for herself and the industry.

While getting a job in the country was difficult, Ibrahimllari started with SHUKALB the year before she graduated, moving to the Korçë utility in southern Albania for her job. While she didn’t have any experience running a wastewater treatment plant, Ibrahimllari was able to work with the plant director to draft an organization chart. The positions were all filled by men, and it took two years to find a woman to fill a position, as not enough were studying necessary roles.

While she was in school, Ibrahimllari had a professor who constantly encouraged her to pursue a role in water utilities. This led to her searching for outreach programs and opportunities within professional networks, such as the Young Water Professionals (YWP). Through this persistence, Ibrahimllari was able to secure a position as project manager at YWP for 8 years.

Ibrahimllari’s drive to succeed also helped her to gain the insight of a mentor in her first internship. Ibrahimllari emphasizes the power of finding a mentor as somebody to “[give you] the opportunity to attend activities and expand [your] knowledge”.

“My degree played a crucial role, and the fact that I could speak English” Ibrahimllari explained. She attributes this and international connections to her continued success in networking and her career.

While Ibrahimllari was successful in pursuing a career in the wastewater sector thanks to her passion, determination, and education, she also pointed out many of the difficulties facing women working in the industry. For example, many potential employees of wastewater utilities face issues like nepotism, a subjective hiring process, and lack of job security due to the absence of a standard set of qualifications needed for a particular job.

"It’s seen like something that 'You can’t do that, you can’t touch that, you are a woman.'"

"I can do that."


To combat this potential hiring bias, Ibrahimllari discussed the possible requirement of certificates and degrees to easily demonstrate a person’s capability to work in the wastewater sector.

Arlinda was the first international participant to take and pass the Professional Operator for Wastewater Treatment Plant exam.

Click play below to hear more!

Ibrahimllari was the first international participant to take and pass the Professional Operator for Wastewater Treatment Plant exam, which she completed in the United States. The Professional Operator program has four different levels (Class I through Class IV) for the water sector, which includes water treatment, water distribution, wastewater collection, and wastewater treatment. It is an example of the type of certification that can create a more objective hiring process.
